Grounded course-building, live

Worked example — build “Introduction to Food Safety”, a 1-month / 12-lecture UG elective, live on a shared screen. You demo each step; participants repeat it on their own laptops straight after.

Before you start

  • Sign in to Gemini (Pro) and NotebookLM on the machine you will screen-share; zoom the text up so it is legible on a call.
  • Keep one browser tab free for opening source links live.
  • Ask participants to open the same two tools on their own laptops and keep 2–3 of their own course PDFs handy.
  • Share the one-page case study and the assignment sheet in the chat at the start.
  • Ask everyone to type “done” in the chat after each hands-on step so you can pace the group.
Total ~2 hours · Demo each step on your shared screen (3–5 min), then give participants 3–5 min to repeat it — watch the chat for “done” before moving on. Steps 1–8 are Gemini; step 9 is the NotebookLM payoff — protect ~30 min for it.
Gemini Pro gathers and drafts fast but ungrounded — it can invent a fact or a citation. NotebookLM then grounds everything in the sources you loaded and cites every claim. Gemini creates; NotebookLM grounds. Nothing is prepared in advance — you find the research and gather the PDFs live, in steps 2 and 4, so the room sees how grounded sourcing is done.
Show the difference every time. For each step, first paste the shallow prompt most people type and let them see the thin, generic result. Then paste the good prompt built as Context · Role · Task · Format — say the four parts aloud. The gap between the two answers is the lesson; the food-safety content just carries it.
1

Write the course objectives

Gemini Pro
Shallow — what most people type
Write course objectives for a food safety course.
Good — Context · Role · Task · Format
Context New undergraduate elective, "Introduction to Food Safety" (2 credits, 2nd year), at an Indian agricultural university. Students come from agriculture, food technology, dairy and veterinary streams — mixed prior exposure, mixed English fluency. Benchmark against how Cornell, UC Davis, Wageningen and Michigan State frame an intro food-safety course, and align to the UGC Learning-Outcomes- based Curriculum Framework (LOCF): course outcomes with Bloom's-level verbs. Role You are an instructional designer who has written LOCF-compliant syllabi. Task Write 6–8 course objectives; under each, 1–2 measurable outcomes that are assessable in this course. Cover biological / chemical / physical hazards, hygiene & HACCP, and the Indian regulatory context. State the prior knowledge you are assuming. Format Numbered objectives, indented outcomes with the Bloom's level in brackets. One page. List the universities and the framework you drew on at the end. Do not invent citations — if unsure a source is real, say so.
Say: the shallow one gives vague, unusable objectives; the CRTF one gives assessable, level-tagged outcomes. Same model, different prompt.
→ objectives & measurable, level-tagged outcomes
2

Find and summarise the leading research

Gemini Pro
Shallow
Summarise food safety research.
Good — CRTF
Context Same course. Before writing content I need an honest map of the field for a mixed 2nd-year Indian audience — and the real sources behind it. Role You are a subject-matter reviewer who separates settled consensus from contested claims. Task Cover (a) the three hazard classes with 2–3 concrete examples each from Indian food systems; (b) the control frameworks — GHP, HACCP, Codex, and FSSAI's role; (c) 5–6 recent authoritative reviews or reports. For each source note what it establishes and where experts disagree. Format Three short sections for (a)–(c), then a source table: title, author / body, year, one-line finding, working link. Prefer open-access. Mark anything you are not confident is real so we verify it in NotebookLM.
Show it live: this is the research step, in front of the room. Open two or three results to prove they are real; refine the prompt if the sources are thin.
→ field map + candidate sources (verify next)
3

Draft the course outline

Gemini Pro
Shallow
Give me a course outline for food safety.
Good — CRTF
Context Using the objectives from step 1 for a 4-week / 12-lecture elective. Role You are a course designer who sequences for a mixed-ability room. Task Design a 4-week module outline with a clear arc — hazards → controls → regulation → application. For each week give the theme, 2–3 key concepts, why it matters to this audience, the misconception to pre-empt, and how it builds on the week before. Format One labelled block per week with those five fields. One page. Concrete themes, not generic headers.
→ 4-week module outline with a build
4

Gather the sources

Gemini Pro
Shallow
Find some papers on food safety.
Good — CRTF
Context Same course; I need citable, mostly free material mapped to the four weekly themes that a mixed Indian 2nd-year audience can actually read. Role You are a research librarian who checks that links resolve and sources are credible. Task For each weekly theme find 2–3 sources: open courseware / syllabi from leading universities, FAO / WHO / Codex or FSSAI guidance, and open-access papers. Prefer Indian context where it exists, and judge the reading level. Format Grouped by week: title, author / body, year, type, reading level (easy / medium / hard), working link. Only list sources you are confident exist; do not fabricate DOIs or links.
Do it live: Gemini returns the list; open 2–3 links to prove they are real, then download 3–4 PDFs. These become the NotebookLM sources in step 9 — generated in front of the room, not handed out.
→ the source list, gathered live by Gemini
5

Build the 12-lecture calendar

Gemini Pro
Shallow
Make a lecture schedule for a food safety course.
Good — CRTF
Context Turning the outline into the schedule — 3 lectures a week for 4 weeks, 60 minutes each, for "Introduction to Food Safety". Role You are the course designer. Task Produce 12 lectures with continuity. Each lecture: a concrete title, one measurable objective (Bloom's verb), the key idea, one in-class activity, and the reading it draws on. Each must depend on the one before; mark the two lectures that carry assessment. Format A table — # | title | objective | key idea | activity | reading.
→ 12-lecture calendar, one objective each
6

Model the students

Gemini Pro
Shallow
Describe the students taking this course.
Good — CRTF
Context This elective runs at an Indian agricultural university. I want to design for the real mix without touching any real student data. Role You are an education researcher building synthetic personas. Task Generate a synthetic profile of 30 students: spread across disciplines and year, prior food-safety exposure (none / some / strong), English comfort, why they chose the elective, and one likely struggle. Then distil 3 personas that between them cover the extremes, each with a teaching implication. Format A compact summary of the 30 (distributions), then 3 named personas. State clearly this is synthetic data, not real students.
Say: we invent students so we design for the real room — safe synthetic data, no real records.
→ 3 learner personas
7

Map each session to the learner

Gemini Pro
Shallow
Map the sessions to the students.
Good — CRTF
Context The 12-lecture calendar, the 3 personas, and the source list. Role You are an instructional designer who thinks from the learner's chair. Task For each of the 12 lectures, map: the one question the student walks in with, the single idea they must walk out with, one example from Indian daily life, the likely misconception, and 1–2 readings from the source list. If a lecture has no strong reading yet, say so plainly. Format One table — # | arrives asking | must leave knowing | everyday example | misconception | readings.
→ per-session learner map with readings
8

Draft the content

Gemini Pro
Shallow — the one-shot trap
Write all twelve lectures for the course now.
Good — one 60-minute lecture at a time
Context Lecture 1 of "Introduction to Food Safety" — a single 60-minute session — for the mixed 2nd-year audience, the personas and readings above. We draft one lecture at a time, on purpose. Role You are the lecturer, writing notes you could teach from tomorrow. Task Draft this one 60-minute lecture only: a 2-minute hook on a real Indian food-safety incident; the core content in 3 beats tied to the readings, timed to fill ~40 minutes; a 10-minute in-class activity; 3 self-check questions with answers; 2 misconceptions to correct. Do not draft any other lecture — stop when this one is done, and I will ask for the next. Format Headed sections with rough timings that sum to 60 minutes. Plain teaching English. Mark any fact that needs a source as [source: …] — we verify next.
Why one at a time: ask for all twelve in one shot and the model spreads thin — the later lectures go generic and quality quietly drops (the failure pattern from Session 1). One 60-minute lecture per prompt keeps each one sharp, and you review before moving on. Repeat for lecture 2, then 3. Decomposition is the quality control.
→ one teachable 60-minute lecture · repeat, one at a time, × 12
9

Consolidate and ground it — the payoff

NotebookLM
  1. Create a notebook, “Introduction to Food Safety”. Add every source gathered in step 4 plus your draft lecture notes from step 8.
  2. Ask a grounded question: “What are the top five foodborne hazards students must understand, with citations?” — click a citation to jump to the exact passage.
  3. Run the trap question: ask for something not in the sources (a made-up statistic). Watch it decline instead of inventing — that is grounding.
  4. Studio → Reports → Study Guide, then Briefing doc. Every claim is cited.
  5. Studio → Quiz and Flashcards — generated only from your sources.
  6. Studio → Audio Overview — the "wow": a spoken summary of the course, from your sources.
  7. Verify live: take one of the [source: …] gaps from step 8, ask it in the notebook, and confirm the answer's citation matches the source. This is the habit to leave the room with.
→ cited study guides, summaries, quizzes and audio · minimal hallucination
