The mental model

A Harvard-educated five-year-old

An LLM is built to follow instructions, and it has read almost the entire library. Treat it as a brilliant colleague who is also five — encyclopaedic, eager, and naive about your world.

It can

Write persuasively, explain hard ideas, draft code, reason through a problem.

It cannot

Guess your context, know when it is wrong, or think beyond the patterns it was trained on.

It cannot understand your context because it has to be told it — it cannot guess. And it is confidently wrong: it will make the mistake and sound certain doing it.

AI for Educators — Session 109
How it was built · what made it work

Test it, and make it work in pieces

It admitted a limit

I asked whether a simple instruction could fully replicate a specialised research tool. It did not just say yes. It said it could only approximate it, and explained that the difference was built in, not something a prompt could fix. That honesty is what makes it worth trusting.

Make it decompose

It wanted to write the whole plan in one shot. I made it work one section at a time. Its own admission: one shot, and the quality quietly drops. That is a failure pattern you can manage.

The skill is the steering — give it a role, demand depth, break the work into pieces, and make it be honest.

There is a way — and a language — to work with this new kind of AI.

AI for Educators — Session 110
By design, not a bug

Ask it to grade — twice

The prompt — run it twice

Q: Define osmosis.   A: Water moving across a membrane.   Rate this answer 1 to 10, 10 is best.

What comes back

First run: 6 / 10  ·  Second run: 8 / 10  — same answer, different verdict.

By design — creativity

It samples among plausible words. That is exactly what gives you ten fresh ways to open a lecture or twenty new exam questions.

For a decision — ground it

A grade must be repeatable. Give it a fixed rubric, explicit criteria, and a second check before you let it decide.

AI for Educators — Session 111
The model landscape

Rent it, or run it yourself

Every model is one of two kinds — proprietary, which you rent, or open weight, which you can download and run.

Proprietary — you rent it

ChatGPT · GPT-5.5 Pro   Claude · Opus 4.8   Gemini · 3.1 Pro

The weights stay with the company. Easiest to start — but your data goes to the vendor.

Open weight — you run it

DeepSeek   Qwen · Alibaba   GLM · Zhipu

The weights are published — run them on your own servers, so data never leaves. Meta's Llama and Mistral too. You need the hardware.

You will start with the proprietary chat tools. Open weight is how a department keeps sensitive data in-house. Names and versions change every few months.

AI for Educators — Session 120
A prediction that failed

The radiologists are still here

In 2016, Geoffrey Hinton — a Nobel laureate and one of the founders of modern AI — said we should stop training radiologists; machines would out-read them within five years.

It did not happen. Radiologists are in short supply, and AI became a tool they use — reading scans faster, catching more — not a replacement.

AI did not replace the experts. The experts who use it are pulling ahead of those who do not. That is the opportunity in this room.

AI for Educators — Session 121
Why the whole world is gung-ho

From giving tasks to giving intent

For seventy years you told a computer the exact steps. Now you can tell it what you want — the intent — and it works out the steps itself.

Traditional computing

You write every instruction. It does exactly that — nothing more, nothing less.

AI so far

You ask a question or show examples; it answers, predicts, or classifies.

Agentic AI

You give it a goal; it plans the steps and acts to reach it.

An agent is an AI you hand a goal — it breaks the goal into tasks, uses tools, and works through them, with you at the gate.

AI for Educators — Session 118
Why this is powerful

Old world, new world

This is the real shift — and it is bigger than it first looks.

Old world — instruction

You spell out every step. Deterministic: the same input gives the same output, every time. Precise and repeatable — but you do all the thinking.

New world — intent

You say what you want; it works out the how. Probabilistic: the same input can give a different output. Far more powerful — and it varies, so it needs steering.

The power is real, and so is the need for judgement. The rest of today is learning where each one fits.

AI for Educators — Session 121
Instruction vs intent — the same task

Tell it what to do, or tell it what you want

You instruct it

Prompt

Write ten multiple-choice questions on today's topic.

What you get

Exactly ten questions, all one difficulty. It followed your words — with no idea whether they test what mattered.

You give it the goal

Prompt

I want to know which students understood today's class. Help me build a way to check.

What you get

It asks what you taught, mixes recall with application, adds the common mistakes to catch, and explains why. It worked toward the goal.

Instruct it and it obeys. Give it a goal and let it reason, and it does more than you asked.

Under the tip · 1 of 3

It can think before it answers

Give it a field-trial calculation. Older tools answered instantly, sometimes wrongly.

Newer models work it out step by step, on a scratchpad you do not see, before they reply. Fewer careless slips on anything with steps — a statistics problem, a multi-part plan.

It reduces careless error. It does not add understanding — a fluent chain of reasoning can still rest on a wrong premise.

It can act, not just answer

Ask it to build a reading list on drip irrigation, group the papers by theme, and lay it out as a table.

An agent is given a goal and works through the steps — search, sort, format — instead of answering one question and stopping. Like handing a task to a capable assistant, not asking across a desk.

More autonomy means more checking, not less — never let it send an email, spend money, or submit a form without a human at the gate.

Skills, tools, and MCP

On its own, a model is a closed book. Three things let it reach into your world.

Skills

A saved way of working. Teach it once how you build a rubric or a report, and it does it your way every time.

Tools

Hands. It can search the web, run a calculation, or read your actual files instead of guessing.

The universal socket — one standard so any tool plugs into any model. What USB did for devices.

Most new capability is the model plus these — not the model alone.

AI for Educators — Session 122
The rules you already work under

What UGC actually says

There is no separate UGC rule for AI. The UGC Academic Integrity Regulations, 2018 already reach it — the same rules that govern plagiarism.

Copied is caught

AI text that reproduces others' work trips the same similarity thresholds — Level 0 up to 10%, then rising penalties past 40%, 60%, and above.

Passed off is misconduct

Presenting AI output as your own original work breaks the regulation's core requirement — original contribution and independent analysis.

The safe practice — disclose AI use, keep it auxiliary, and verify before you submit.

AI for Educators — Session 129
The moral line

The rules are the floor — judgement is the rest

Misuse is the issue, not use. Six habits keep you on the right side of it.

Disclose

Say when AI materially shaped the work.

Keep it auxiliary

The ideas, and the authorship, stay yours.

Verify

Check anything feeding a grade, a decision, or a report.

Protect data

No student records or unpublished work in public tools.

Expect bias

It reflects its training and is confidently wrong.

Stay accountable

It goes out under your name — you own it.

The sharp edge: in a DPR that rides real funding, the risk is an unverified AI fact, not copied text. Verification and accountability are human.

AI for Educators — Session 126
Responsible use

The principle we leave with

AI is not banned, and it is not a shortcut around thinking. The tool drafts and speeds the work; the ideas, the verification and the accountability stay with us.

For reports and DPRs that inform real funding decisions, the risk is not copied text — it is an unverified, AI-generated fact riding a decision.

Verification and accountability are the controls — and both are human.

Disclose

Say when AI helped produce the work.

Verify

Check every fact, figure and citation yourself.

Protect data

Keep IP and personal data out of public tools.

Stay accountable

The author owns the output, not the tool.
