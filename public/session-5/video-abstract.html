AI for Educators · Session 5 · Video abstracts

Build a video abstract, end to end — run it live, then hand it over

Every step below is a ready-to-paste Gemini prompt, already filled with our worked example. Run them in order in front of the room to produce a complete ~2-minute video-abstract plan. Then attendees repeat the same steps on their own paper, using the blank assignment at the end.
What a video abstract is. A short audio-and-visual summary of a paper, in the author's own words, telling people why they should read it — made to carry research beyond its own field and onto social channels. Publishers put the sweet spot at about two minutes (Taylor & Francis: 2 minutes 20 seconds or less; SAGE: ~2-minute animation). A strong one answers three questions: what did you investigate, how did you do it, and what did you conclude — then points the viewer to the full article. Ours below follows exactly that arc. Reference: Taylor & Francis — creating a video abstract.
The golden rule — say it before you start. Gemini only ever sees a plain, generic summary: aim, method in words, one headline result. Raw data, exact unpublished figures, and your novel design stay out. Every number here is a dummy you swap for your own verified value at the very end.
The habit we are teaching — let the model ask. People forget to say who the audience is, which venue it is for, or how long it can be. So every prompt below ends by telling Gemini to ask you for anything missing before it generates. That one line turns a guessing model into a collaborator that gets it right the first time.
Our target venue (real, worked example): a SAGE-style journal video abstract. Roughly a two-minute animated video that highlights the article's key findings, with narration; the script is approved by the author and the video is peer-reviewed before publication; it is made for social dissemination and for reaching decision-makers. Watch real samples first: languageservices.sagepub.com — video abstracts.

The example finding (illustrative — everyone builds this one in class)

Small farmers lose much of their tomato harvest to spoilage before sale, because there is no affordable cold storage. A zero-energy cool chamber — two brick walls with damp sand between them, no electricity — keeps tomatoes cooler and more humid, so they stay market-ready for extra days.

Headline result (plain): the chamber cut spoilage by roughly 60% over ten days versus open storage.

⚠ 60% is a dummy figure for teaching. Swap in your own real, verified number before you publish anything.

The six steps you will run

  1. Brainstorm the angle. Generate several ways to open; pick the strongest hook.
  2. Lock the venue spec. Turn the SAGE guidelines into a concrete build spec — audience, tone, 2-minute length, must-haves.
  3. Break up the story and its elements. Map the narrative arc and the visual and number for each beat.
  4. Storyboard. Scene by scene, timed to two minutes: shot, on-screen text, narration beat, seconds.
  5. Frame and script. The full ~2-minute narration and the shot framing for each scene.
  6. Reviewer pass — change the role. A fresh, independent reviewer does a gap analysis against the SAGE criteria. You fix and finalise.
1

Brainstorm the angle

Gemini · creative partner
Run this live · paste as-is — it is already seeded with our example
A video abstract lives or dies on its opening. Do not settle on the first idea — ask for several, then choose. Notice this prompt gives the finding but not the audience or venue on purpose, so you can watch Gemini ask for them.
▸ Prompt to paste
Context I have a paper on cutting post-harvest tomato losses for small farmers. Plain summary, no sensitive data — aim: help smallholders store tomatoes without electricity; method: a low-cost evaporative cool chamber (two brick walls with damp sand between them); headline result: spoilage cut by about 60% over ten days versus open storage (dummy figure). I am making a ~2-minute video abstract. Role You are a science-storytelling partner who makes research memorable. Task Give me 4 distinct angles for the video, each opening on a different hook (a human moment, a surprising number, a question, a visual contrast). For each: the hook line, the through-line, and who it lands with best. Format Four short blocks; end with a one-line recommendation. Before you generate: if the target venue, the audience, or the length is unclear or missing, ask me those questions first and wait for my answers.
▸ What Gemini does first — it asks
Gemini replies with questions before drafting
  1. Which venue is this for, so I match its length and style?
  2. Who is the primary audience — researchers, practitioners, funders, or students?
  3. Is ~2 minutes a hard limit, or a target?
You answer: a SAGE journal video abstract; audience is mixed researchers and practitioners; two minutes is the target. → Gemini now proceeds.
▸ Sample output from Gemini
A · The human moment. Open on a farmer watching tomatoes rot. Through-line: dignity and lost income. Lands with practitioners and funders.
B · The surprising number. Open on "~60% less spoilage — with zero electricity." Through-line: big result, near-zero cost. Lands with a results-driven research audience.
C · The question. Open on "Can you refrigerate food with no power at all?" Through-line: simple physics answering a real problem. Lands with a general and student audience.
D · The contrast. Open on two tomatoes at Day 10, side by side. Through-line: seeing is believing. Lands where visual proof matters most.
Recommended: open on A (human moment) for SAGE's decision-maker reach, but bring the ~60% result in within the first 20 seconds.
2

Lock the venue spec

Gemini · analyst
Run this live · SAGE guidelines already pasted in
The same finding is pitched differently to a journal, a conference, and a funder. Here we turn SAGE's real guidelines into a written build spec so every later step is tuned to it.
▸ Prompt to paste
Context I am submitting this video abstract to a SAGE journal. Its guidelines, in my words: a ~2-minute animated video that highlights the article's key findings, with narration; the script is approved by me before animation; the video is peer-reviewed before publication; it is meant for social dissemination and for reaching decision-makers. Chosen angle from Step 1: open on the human moment, bring the ~60% result in early. Role You are an analyst who turns a call for entries into a build spec. Task Produce a one-page spec: Audience, Tone, Length, Must-have elements, and what this format is judged/valued on. Then say how my chosen angle should adapt. Format A labelled spec, then a short "how to adapt the angle" note. If anything you need is not in my summary, ask me before you generate.
▸ Sample output from Gemini

Audience: researchers and practitioners; secondarily decision-makers on social channels.
Tone: credible and warm — evidence first, human impact close behind; accessible, minimal jargon.
Length: about 2 minutes (~240–260 narration words).
Must-have: the key finding stated clearly, the method shown, real-world relevance, author-approved script.
Valued on: clarity, scientific soundness, relevance and reach, production quality.

Adapt the angle: open on the human moment for reach, but state the ~60% result plainly within the first 20 seconds to satisfy scientific soundness; keep the method visibly animated.

This is the hyper-personalisation. Every step from here is built to this spec, not to a generic idea of a good video.
3

Break up the story and its elements

Gemini · story editor
Run this live · feed it Steps 1–2 output
Before storyboarding, split the piece into a narrative arc, and for each beat name the concrete element that carries it — the visual, the on-screen number, the one message.
▸ Prompt to paste
Context For the tomato cool-chamber finding (spoilage down ~60% over ten days, dummy), using the chosen angle and the SAGE spec from Steps 1–2 (paste them below this line if starting a fresh chat). Role You are a story editor for short science films. Task Break the ~2 minutes into a beat sheet: hook, problem, approach, how it works, result, impact, close. For each beat give the single message, the visual element, and any on-screen number. One idea per beat. Format Seven beats, each: Message · Visual · On-screen. End with the one line the viewer should remember. If the angle or spec is missing, ask me for it before you generate.
▸ Sample output from Gemini
Hook — Message: every harvest, food is lost. Visual: farmer beside softening tomatoes. On-screen: "Part of every harvest — lost".
Problem — Message: the cause is no cold storage. Visual: empty farm, no power line. On-screen: "No electricity, no cold storage".
Approach — Message: a chamber cooled by evaporation. Visual: bricks, sand, water packed in. On-screen: "A zero-energy cool chamber".
How it works — Message: evaporation carries heat away. Visual: animated arrows, water leaving, heat out. On-screen: "Evaporation = cooling".
Result — Message: it works, by a lot. Visual: Day-10 side by side. On-screen: "Day 10 · ~60% less spoilage · vs open storage".
Impact — Message: more days to sell, more income. Visual: farmer selling fresh fruit. On-screen: "Less waste. More income."
Close — Message: simple physics, near-zero cost. Visual: finished chamber on the farm. On-screen: title + affiliation.

Remember-line: a refrigerator with no electricity — built from a farm's own materials.

5

Frame and script

Gemini · scriptwriter
Run this live · feed it the storyboard
Finally, the spoken narration and a framing note per scene — so whoever animates or shoots knows exactly what to capture, timed to two minutes.
▸ Prompt to paste
Context From the storyboard below, write the final narration and framing. STORYBOARD: [paste from Step 4] Role You are a scriptwriter and director of photography for short films. Task Write a ~250-word narration (about 2 minutes spoken) in plain language, and for each scene a one-line framing note (angle, what fills the frame). Keep the ~60% result (dummy) as the emotional peak. Format The narration as a paragraph, then a short framing list, scene by scene. If the storyboard or target length is missing, ask me before you generate.
▸ Sample output from Gemini
"Across India, small farmers grow tomatoes in abundance — and then watch a large share of that harvest rot before it ever reaches a buyer. The problem is not the growing. It is the waiting. Without cold storage, a ripe tomato has only a few days before it softens, and for a smallholder with no electricity, cold storage is simply out of reach. So we asked a simple question: could you keep tomatoes cool using no electricity at all? Our answer is the zero-energy cool chamber — two brick walls with a layer of damp sand packed between them. As water evaporates from the sand, it draws heat away from the space inside, the same physics that cools your skin as sweat dries. A refrigerator, in effect, built from materials already on the farm. To test it, we stored freshly picked tomatoes inside and compared them with tomatoes left in open storage, measuring both over ten days. The result was striking: the chamber cut spoilage by around sixty percent, keeping the fruit firm, red and market-ready for days longer. For a smallholder, those extra days change everything — less waste, a wider window to sell, and more income, from a structure that costs almost nothing. This is not advanced technology. It is careful physics, applied where it is needed most."
Framing — Hook: close on soft fruit, farmer soft-focus behind. Problem: wide empty farm at dusk. Approach: mid shots of hands and sand. Diagram: clean full-frame animation. Result: locked-off two-shot, identical framing on both tomatoes. Impact: warm market shot, real sale. Close: slow pull-back from the finished chamber.
You do the rest: this script and storyboard now go into your video tool (next box). Gemini only ever saw the plain summary — never your raw data.
R

Reviewer pass — change the role, find the gaps

Gemini · independent reviewer
Run this live · open a fresh chat and change the role
Now change the role deliberately. In a new chat, tell Gemini it is an independent reviewer seeing this for the first time — not the author. Fresh eyes catch the unclear hook, the jargon, the late result, the missing must-have. This adversarial pass lifts the piece from good to competitive.
▸ Prompt to paste
Context Below is a video-abstract storyboard and script, and the SAGE spec it must satisfy. You did not write it and know nothing about the project beyond this. SPEC: [paste from Step 2] STORYBOARD + SCRIPT: [paste from Steps 4–5] Role You are an independent reviewer and journal editor, slightly sceptical, scoring this against the venue's stated criteria. Task Do a gap analysis. For each criterion, say where it falls short and what is missing, unclear, jargon-heavy, or overclaimed. Flag any claim I have not evidenced. Be specific and blunt. Format A table: Criterion · Gap found · Fix. End with the single highest-priority change. If the spec or the draft is incomplete, ask me for it before you review.
▸ Sample output from Gemini
CriterionGap foundFix
Clarity"Evaporation = cooling" assumes the viewer knows why.Keep the one narration line about heat leaving as water dries.
Scientific soundness"~60%" needs its comparison and duration on screen, not only spoken.On-screen: "vs open storage · 10 days".

Close the loop: take these fixes back to Step 4 or 5 and revise. One or two review-and-fix rounds is usually enough — you own every final decision.

Turn the script into an actual video

Gemini has given you a finished script and storyboard. It does not render the video itself, so the last step uses a video tool. Any of these works — pick what your team has:

  1. Google Vids or Canva: create a scene per storyboard row, drop in the on-screen text, add simple visuals, then add the narration as a voiceover track.
  2. NotebookLM Video Overview: load your safe summary and script as sources and generate a narrated video from them — grounded in exactly what you provided.
  3. Any AI clip tool (e.g. Veo in the Gemini app): generate short clips scene by scene from the storyboard, then stitch and narrate.

Then record the narration (or use text-to-speech), lay it over the visuals, and check it against the reviewer's fixes. That is your video abstract.

Export settings publishers expect: 16:9 landscape, at least 720p, 24–30 fps, saved as .mp4 (.mov or .mpg also accepted), kept under about 2 minutes 20 seconds. End on a line that points the viewer to the full article.

Save the whole flow as a Gem in Gemini

Do not rebuild this by hand each time. In Gemini, a Gem is a saved assistant with standing instructions — set it up once, reuse it for every paper.

  • Create a Gem named "Video-abstract builder".
  • Paste the flow as its instructions: the golden rule, then the six steps with their CRTF structure.
  • Tell it to run one step at a time, to ask for anything missing before each step, and to mark unverified facts and dummy numbers.
  • For a new paper, open the Gem, paste your safe summary and the venue's guidelines, and walk the steps.

Why it matters: the Gem locks in the discipline — decomposition, venue-tuning, the asking habit, and the independent review — so quality does not depend on remembering the process.
