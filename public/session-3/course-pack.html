Introduction to Food Safety — Course Pack

A one-month, 12-lecture undergraduate elective. Drafted with Gemini Pro following the AI-for-Educators run sheet, to be grounded and explored in NotebookLM. Claims that need evidence are marked [source: …] so they can be verified against the readings.

1. Course objectives

By the end of this course, a student can:

  1. Explain why food safety matters and the burden of foodborne illness, globally and in India.
  2. Identify biological, chemical and physical hazards and where they enter the food chain.
  3. Apply the principles of food hygiene and HACCP to a simple process.
  4. Describe the roles of Codex, FAO/WHO and FSSAI in food safety.
  5. Read basic food-safety evidence and data critically.
  6. Evaluate a real food operation and propose control measures.

2. Research brief — the field in one page

  • Hazard classes. Food hazards fall into three families: biological (bacteria, viruses, parasites), chemical (contaminants, residues, adulterants, natural toxins) and physical (stones, metal, glass) — with allergens as a special case [source: Hazard risk review].
  • Control frameworks. Good Hygiene Practices are the base; HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points) builds on top; Codex Alimentarius sets the global baseline; FSSAI is the Indian regulator [source: Codex hygiene] [source: FSSAI manual].
  • Scale. Foodborne illness is a large, mostly hidden burden that falls hardest on children and the vulnerable; recent global-burden work updates the picture [source: WHO fact sheet] [source: Lancet burden].
  • Where sources differ. Exact burden numbers vary by method and year; adoption of HACCP in small/informal units is contested and often weak [source: HACCP barriers]. Verify all figures in NotebookLM before teaching them.

Lecture 1: The burden of unsafe food

Objective: Explain the global and Indian scale of foodborne illness in a way students can feel, not just memorise.

Hook (2 min): Think about the last wedding or mela you attended. Hundreds of people ate from the same kitchen. Now imagine the biryani sat out in the sun a little too long. Sometimes nothing happens; sometimes half the guests are unwell by night. Unsafe food is not a rare accident, it is a large and mostly hidden burden on human health. WHO estimates that a very large number of people fall ill every year from eating contaminated food [source: WHO fact sheet].

Core content (~40 min):

Food becomes "unsafe" when it carries something that can harm us: bacteria, viruses, parasites, or chemical contaminants. When people eat such food, they can get foodborne diseases, ranging from a day of loose motions to serious, life-threatening illness. The important point for this course is scale. Foodborne illness is not just the occasional bad meal; it is a steady, everyday load of sickness across the whole population. WHO reports that hundreds of millions of people worldwide fall ill each year, and that a share of these cases end in death [source: WHO fact sheet].

This burden is not shared equally. Children under five, older people, pregnant women, and those already weak or unwell are hit hardest. A bout of diarrhoea that an adult shrugs off can be dangerous for a small child. WHO highlights that children, especially in low- and middle-income regions, carry a heavy portion of the foodborne disease burden [source: WHO fact sheet]. Recent work looking at the global burden across many foodborne hazards over the past two decades gives us a clearer, updated picture of who suffers and how much [source: Lancet burden].

For India, this matters a great deal. We are a large population with a warm climate, long supply chains, a big informal food sector (think street vendors, small dairies, roadside dhabas), and uneven access to clean water and cold storage. All of these conditions make it easier for food to spoil or get contaminated before it reaches the plate. When we say food safety "matters," we mean it prevents a large amount of avoidable illness, lost school and work days, and medical cost, especially for the poorest families.

In-class activity (10 min): In pairs, each student lists three foods they ate in the last 24 hours and marks, for each, one point where it could have become unsafe (dirty water, no refrigeration, unwashed hands, old stock). Then 3-4 pairs share one example aloud, and the class groups them into "biological" versus "chemical" causes on the board.

Self-check: 1. What makes a food "unsafe"? — It carries a hazard (microbe, parasite, or chemical) that can harm the person who eats it. 2. Who is most vulnerable to foodborne illness? — Young children, the elderly, pregnant women, and people who are already sick or weak. 3. Name two Indian conditions that raise foodborne risk. — Warm climate and long/informal supply chains with limited cold storage and clean water.

Common misconceptions: - "Food poisoning is just a rare, minor upset." — It is common and can be serious or fatal, especially for children [source: WHO fact sheet]. - "Only poor or dirty places have this problem." — Unsafe food occurs everywhere; risk depends on handling and conditions, not just on how a place looks.

Readings: [WHO fact sheet]; [Lancet burden]

Lecture 2: From farm to fork

Objective: Map where food-safety risks arise along the chain, from production to the plate.

Hook (2 min): Picture a glass of milk on your breakfast table. Before it reached you, it travelled through a cow, a milking shed, a can, a transport vehicle, a chilling centre, a shop, and finally your kitchen. A mistake at any one of these steps can make that safe-looking glass unsafe. Today we trace that whole journey.

Core content (~40 min):

Food safety is not decided at one moment; it is built (or broken) all along a chain we call "farm to fork." The main links are production (the farm, dairy, or fishery), processing, storage and transport, retail or the vendor, and finally preparation in the home or hotel. At each link, hazards can enter or multiply. A useful idea from WHO is that safe food depends on everyone in this chain playing their part, from the farmer to the final cook [source: WHO fact sheet].

Let us look at where things typically go wrong. On the farm, risks include contaminated water, animal disease, and misuse of pesticides or veterinary medicines that leave residues. During processing and transport, risks include dirty equipment, mixing of clean and unclean batches, and, very importantly, breaks in the cold chain, when perishable food gets warm enough for microbes to multiply. At the vendor and home stage, the big risks are dirty hands, unclean water, cross-contamination between raw and cooked food, and reheating that is too gentle to kill germs. The broad picture of which hazards cause the most harm helps us decide where to focus effort [source: Lancet burden].

The key teaching point is that risk is cumulative and often invisible. Food does not announce that it has been mishandled; it can look, smell, and taste normal and still be unsafe. This is why food safety is a shared responsibility and why we manage the whole chain rather than only inspecting the final product. In the Indian context, an unbroken cold chain for milk, fish, and meat, and clean water at the vendor's cart, are two of the highest-value points to protect.

In-class activity (10 min): As a class, build a "farm to fork" chain on the board for one food chosen by students (milk, chicken, or a plate of chaat). For each link, students call out one hazard and one simple control. The instructor marks which link they think is the weakest and asks the class to vote.

Self-check: 1. What does "farm to fork" mean? — The full chain a food travels, from production through processing, transport, and retail to final preparation and eating. 2. What is a "break in the cold chain"? — When perishable food gets too warm during storage or transport, letting microbes multiply. 3. Why can't we judge safety by looking at food? — Contaminated food often looks, smells, and tastes normal.

Common misconceptions: - "If it looks and smells fine, it is safe." — Many hazards leave no visible or smell signal. - "Food safety is only the cook's job." — Every link in the chain, from farmer to consumer, affects safety [source: WHO fact sheet].

Readings: [WHO fact sheet]; [Lancet burden]

Lecture 3: What "safe" means

Objective: Define hazard, risk, and safety in precise food terms, and show how they differ.

Hook (2 min): If I ask, "Is street food safe?", most of you will argue both yes and no, and you will all be partly right. The reason is that "safe" is not a simple yes/no word. To use it well, we need three sharper words: hazard, risk, and safety. By the end of today you will not confuse them again.

Core content (~40 min):

A hazard is a thing that can cause harm. In food, hazards fall into three families: biological (bacteria, viruses, parasites), chemical (pesticide residues, heavy metals, natural toxins, adulterants), and physical (stones, glass, metal pieces). A hazard is about potential, the sharp edge of a knife is a hazard whether or not it ever cuts you. The updated global burden work is essentially a large accounting of many such food hazards and the harm they cause [source: Lancet burden].

Risk is different: it is the chance that a hazard will actually cause harm, together with how serious that harm would be. So risk combines two things, how likely and how bad. A hazard present in tiny amounts, handled carefully, may pose low risk; the same hazard in large amounts, or reaching a vulnerable child, poses high risk. This is why we can say a hazard "exists" while the risk stays low, if it is well controlled. Understanding this distinction is the foundation of every food-safety decision.

That leads us to the definition of safety. Food safety does not mean zero hazards, which is impossible in the real world; it means keeping risk at an acceptable, low level so that food will not harm the consumer when prepared and eaten as intended. WHO frames food safety around preventing contamination and controlling hazards throughout the chain so that food is safe to eat [source: WHO fact sheet]. In short: we cannot remove every hazard, but through good practices we can manage risk down to a level that protects people. This management-of-risk idea is the thread that runs through the rest of this course.

In-class activity (10 min): Give students five short scenario cards (e.g., "a nail found in bread," "milk left out for six hours," "traces of pesticide within safe limits," "raw chicken juice dripping on a salad," "a cracked but clean plate"). In small groups, they label each as hazard type and rate the risk as low/medium/high, then defend one rating to the class.

Self-check: 1. What is the difference between a hazard and a risk? — A hazard is something that can cause harm; risk is the chance it actually will, plus how serious the harm would be. 2. Name the three families of food hazards. — Biological, chemical, and physical. 3. Does "safe food" mean zero hazards? — No; it means risk is controlled down to an acceptably low level so food does not harm the eater.

Common misconceptions: - "Hazard and risk are the same thing." — A hazard is potential harm; risk is the likelihood and severity of that harm occurring. - "Safe means completely free of any hazard." — Safety means acceptable, well-controlled risk, not the impossible goal of zero hazards.

Readings: [WHO fact sheet]; [Lancet burden]

Lecture 4: Biological hazards — the living things that make food unsafe

Objective: Identify key foodborne pathogens and understand how they spread from farm to plate.

Hook (2 min): Think of a wedding or a temple bhandara where hundreds eat the same rice and dal, and by night many families are visiting the doctor with vomiting and loose motions. Nobody added poison. The culprit was invisible: tiny living organisms that multiplied in food left warm for too long. Large shared meals are a common setting for foodborne outbreaks [source: Hazard risk review]. Today we meet these invisible troublemakers.

Core content (~40 min): Biological hazards are living or once-living agents that cause illness when we eat them: bacteria, viruses, parasites, and sometimes moulds. Not every microbe is harmful, but a specific group of "pathogens" is. The important bacterial names to know include Salmonella, E. coli, Listeria, Campylobacter, and Staphylococcus; viruses such as norovirus and hepatitis A also travel through food and water. These agents differ in where they live and how sick they make us [source: Hazard risk review]. In the Indian setting, think of Salmonella in undercooked eggs and chicken, or contaminated water splashing onto cut fruit at a roadside stall.

The second beat is how they spread. Pathogens move by a few simple routes: dirty hands, unclean water, raw meat juices dripping onto ready-to-eat food (cross-contamination), pests and flies, and food kept in the "danger zone" of warm temperatures where bacteria double quickly. A food handler who does not wash hands after using the toilet can pass norovirus or hepatitis A to everyone he serves. Poor hygiene, unsafe water, and improper temperature control are repeatedly identified as major drivers of contamination [source: Hazard risk review].

The third beat is why this list keeps changing. New or "emerging" pathogens appear because of changes in how we farm, trade, and store food, longer supply chains, climate shifts, and the growing problem of antibiotic resistance, which makes some infections harder to treat. Some organisms once thought harmless are now recognised as dangerous, and familiar ones show up in new foods [source: Emerging pathogens]. This is why a food safety officer must keep learning and cannot rely only on an old fixed list.

In-class activity (10 min): "Trace the germ." In small mixed-background groups, take one everyday Indian food chain — for example, chicken curry at a hostel mess, or cut watermelon from a cart. Draw the journey from source to mouth and mark every point where a biological hazard could enter (water, hands, storage temperature, raw-cooked contact, flies). Each group shares its two riskiest points.

Self-check: 1. Name three groups of biological hazards. — Bacteria, viruses, and parasites (moulds also count). 2. What is cross-contamination? — When pathogens move from a raw food (like raw chicken) to a ready-to-eat food, often through hands, boards, or dripping juices. 3. Why do new foodborne pathogens keep emerging? — Because of changes in farming and trade, longer supply chains, climate, and antibiotic resistance [source: Emerging pathogens].

Common misconceptions: - "If food smells fine, it is safe." Wrong — many dangerous pathogens leave no smell, taste, or colour change. - "Only street food makes people sick." Wrong — home kitchens and large catered events are also frequent sources when hygiene or temperature control slips.

Readings: [Hazard risk review]; [Emerging pathogens]

Lecture 5: Chemical hazards — contaminants, residues and adulterants

Objective: Recognise the main chemical hazards in food: environmental contaminants, agricultural and veterinary residues, and deliberate adulterants.

Hook (2 min): You have surely heard an elder say, "Wash the vegetables well, who knows what they sprayed." That worry is about chemical hazards. Unlike bacteria, these do not multiply, and often you cannot wash them all away or cook them off. Chemical contamination of food is a recognised risk to health alongside microbial hazards [source: Hazard risk review]. Today we learn to tell the three kinds apart.

Core content (~40 min): The first family is environmental and natural contaminants — chemicals that enter food from the surroundings or form naturally. These include heavy metals such as lead, arsenic, and mercury that reach crops and fish through soil and water, and natural toxins made by moulds, called mycotoxins, for example aflatoxin that can grow on badly stored groundnuts, maize, and chillies in humid conditions. Such contaminants are assessed as chemical hazards in food safety [source: Hazard risk review]. The lesson for us: safe storage — dry, cool, pest-free — is not just about spoilage, it prevents toxin formation.

The second family is residues from farming and animal rearing. Pesticides sprayed on crops and veterinary drugs like antibiotics given to poultry, dairy, and fish animals can leave traces in the final food if withdrawal periods and correct doses are not respected. This is a direct concern for our agriculture, dairy, and veterinary students. Managing agricultural and veterinary chemical inputs is part of controlling food-related chemical risk [source: Hazard risk review], and misuse of antibiotics in animals also feeds the larger problem of resistance [source: Emerging pathogens].

The third family is adulteration — chemicals or cheaper substances added on purpose to cheat, usually to increase weight, colour, or shelf life. Common Indian examples discussed in food safety include milk stretched with water or foreign substances, turmeric or spices brightened with unapproved dyes, and edible oils mixed with cheaper oils. Deliberate adulteration is a food safety and quality concern requiring assessment and control [source: Hazard risk review]. The key difference from residues is intent: adulteration is a human choice to deceive.

In-class activity (10 min): "Three baskets." Give groups a mixed set of situations on slips — e.g., aflatoxin on stored groundnut, antibiotic in milk, water added to milk, arsenic in tube-well water reaching rice, banned dye in sweets. Sort each slip into one basket: contaminant, residue, or adulterant. Groups defend one tricky call to the class.

Self-check: 1. How is a residue different from an adulterant? — A residue is an unintended leftover from farming or animal treatment; an adulterant is added deliberately to cheat. 2. Give one example of a natural chemical hazard. — Aflatoxin (a mycotoxin) on poorly stored groundnuts or maize. 3. Why can't you always fix chemical hazards by washing or cooking? — Many chemicals do not break down or wash off; some, like certain toxins and metals, stay in the food.

Common misconceptions: - "Chemical hazards grow in food like germs do." Wrong — chemicals do not multiply; the amount depends on how much entered the food. - "Natural means safe." Wrong — some of the most dangerous food chemicals, like mycotoxins, are entirely natural.

Readings: [Hazard risk review]; [Emerging pathogens]

Lecture 6: Physical hazards and allergens — the ones you can often see, and the ones you cannot

Objective: Spot common physical hazards in food and identify the major food allergens.

Hook (2 min): Imagine biting into a laddu at a function and cracking your tooth on a small stone, or a child eating a besan sweet and suddenly breaking out in hives and struggling to breathe. Two very different dangers — one you can see, one hidden inside the recipe. Both belong to today's class.

Core content (~40 min): Physical hazards are solid foreign objects that get into food and can cut, choke, or break teeth. Typical culprits are stones and grit in pulses and rice, metal pieces from worn machinery, glass shards, plastic, and even staff-related items like hair, buttons, or bits of jewellery. They usually enter during harvesting, milling, processing, or careless handling. Foreign material in food is recognised as a hazard to be controlled [source: Hazard risk review]. Good practices — sieving, cleaning grains, magnets and filters on machines, and simple discipline about not wearing loose ornaments in the kitchen — remove most of them.

Allergens are a special hazard because the food is not "dirty" or "spoiled" at all; it is a normal ingredient that a certain person's body reacts to strongly. Even a tiny amount can trigger a severe, sometimes life-threatening reaction called anaphylaxis. The internationally recognised major allergen groups include milk, eggs, fish, shellfish (crustaceans), tree nuts, peanuts (groundnut), wheat (gluten), and soy; sesame is also widely listed. For our students, groundnut, milk, wheat, and sesame are everyday examples. Because allergens cannot be cooked out, the real controls are honest labelling, avoiding accidental mixing during preparation, and clear communication to the eater [source: Hazard risk review].

Tying the week together: physical hazards are mostly a problem of cleanliness and equipment, while allergens are a problem of information and separation. A useful habit for a food safety professional is to think about all three hazard types — biological, chemical, and physical (with allergens as a special case) — for any food product, because the same dish can carry more than one. Systematic hazard assessment across these categories is the foundation of keeping food safe [source: Hazard risk review].

In-class activity (10 min): "Read the label." Bring wrappers of common packaged Indian snacks (biscuits, namkeen, chocolate). In pairs, students find the allergen information, list which major allergens are declared, and note any "may contain traces" warning. Each pair reports one product and who should avoid it.

Self-check: 1. Give two examples of physical hazards in grains. — Stones or grit, and metal fragments from milling (glass or plastic also acceptable). 2. Why are allergens different from other hazards? — The food itself is normal and clean; only certain people react, and even a small amount can be dangerous. 3. Name four major allergens. — For example groundnut (peanut), milk, egg, and wheat (others: fish, shellfish, tree nuts, soy, sesame).

Common misconceptions: - "A small amount of an allergen is harmless." Wrong — for a sensitive person even a trace can cause a severe reaction. - "Physical hazards are always visible, so they're not serious." Wrong — small stones, metal, or glass are easy to miss and can cause real injury.

Readings: [Hazard risk review]; [Emerging pathogens]

Lecture 7: Good hygiene practices

Objective: Apply the core principles of food hygiene.

Hook (2 min): Think about a busy roadside chaat stall in the evening. The same hand takes cash, wipes the counter, and then serves papdi. Nothing looks dirty, yet a stomach upset the next morning is common. Today we ask a simple question: what actually keeps food safe, and what only looks clean? (If you use a real reported outbreak here, mark it [source: ] — do not state numbers from memory.)

Core content (~40 min):

Food hygiene is not one big rule; it is a set of everyday habits that stop harmful microbes and dirt from reaching food. The starting idea is that hazards can be biological (bacteria, viruses), chemical (pesticide residue, cleaning agents), or physical (glass, hair, stone). Good hygiene works along the whole chain, from the farm and the market to the kitchen and the plate. The Codex General Principles set out this "primary production to consumer" approach, so that no single stage is treated as the only place where safety happens [source: Codex hygiene].

The second beat is the small number of principles that do most of the work. Keep clean: wash hands with soap at the right moments, and clean and sanitise surfaces and utensils. Separate raw and cooked: never let raw chicken juices touch a cut salad. Cook thoroughly and keep hot food hot, cold food cold, because the "danger zone" of warm temperatures lets bacteria multiply fast. Use safe water and safe raw materials. These ideas map onto the Codex Good Hygiene Practices, which are meant as the foundation that must be in place before any more advanced system is added [source: Codex hygiene].

The third beat is people and place. Personal hygiene matters: clean clothing, tied hair, covered cuts, and staying away from food work when ill with vomiting or diarrhoea. The premises matter too: pest control, drainage, waste handling, and a layout that separates dirty and clean areas. Codex frames these as prerequisite programmes — the base every food business needs regardless of size, whether it is a village dairy cooperative or a packaged snacks unit [source: Codex hygiene].

In-class activity (10 min): In pairs, students draw the journey of a glass of packaged buttermilk from the dairy to a shop fridge. At each step they mark one hygiene hazard and one control. Two pairs share; the class checks whether the controls are "keep clean, separate, cook/chill, safe water" in disguise.

Self-check: 1. Name the three types of food hazard. — Biological, chemical, physical. 2. Why keep raw and cooked food apart? — To prevent cross-contamination, where microbes from raw food transfer to ready-to-eat food. 3. What is a "prerequisite programme"? — The basic hygiene conditions (cleaning, pest control, personal hygiene) that must exist before advanced controls like HACCP.

Common misconceptions: - "If it looks clean, it is safe." — Harmful microbes are invisible; safety depends on correct practice, not appearance. - "Hygiene is only the cook's job." — It runs across the whole chain, from grower and transporter to seller and consumer [source: Codex hygiene].

Readings: [Codex hygiene]

Lecture 8: HACCP, step by step

Objective: Work through the seven HACCP principles.

Hook (2 min): Suppose a milk plant waits for the final packed product to test it for bacteria. If the test fails, the whole batch is already made and must be dumped — costly and too late. HACCP flips this thinking: instead of catching problems at the end, we build safety into each step. (If you cite a specific recall as motivation, mark it [source: ].)

Core content (~40 min):

HACCP stands for Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points. It is a preventive, systematic way to manage food safety by focusing effort where it matters most, rather than relying on end-product testing alone. It is built on top of good hygiene practices, not instead of them — the prerequisites from Lecture 7 must already be working [source: HACCP review]. Reviews of HACCP in food and agricultural systems describe it as a structured framework that links hazard thinking to specific, monitored controls [source: HACCP review].

The seven principles are the heart of it. One: conduct a hazard analysis. Two: determine the critical control points (CCPs). Three: establish critical limits for each CCP, such as a minimum temperature. Four: set up a monitoring system. Five: establish corrective actions for when a limit is not met. Six: establish verification procedures to confirm the system works. Seven: keep records and documentation. We will practise CCPs in detail next lecture; today the goal is to see the seven as one connected sequence [source: HACCP review].

The third beat is why HACCP sometimes stays on paper. Studies of adoption, especially in the meat industry, point to real barriers: cost of setting up, shortage of trained staff, weak record-keeping habits, and limited support for small operators [source: HACCP barriers]. This matters in India, where much food handling happens in small and informal units. Understanding barriers is part of understanding the system — a plan that ignores who has to run it tends to fail [source: HACCP barriers].

In-class activity (10 min): Give groups a shuffled set of cards naming the seven principles. They arrange them in order and, for one chosen step in making paneer, write a single example (for instance, a critical limit for pasteurisation). Groups compare orderings and discuss any disagreements.

Self-check: 1. What does HACCP stand for? — Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points. 2. Which principle comes first, and why? — Hazard analysis, because you cannot control hazards you have not identified. 3. Name one commonly reported barrier to HACCP adoption. — Cost, lack of trained staff, or weak record-keeping [source: HACCP barriers].

Common misconceptions: - "HACCP replaces basic hygiene." — It builds on good hygiene practices; prerequisites must already be in place [source: HACCP review]. - "HACCP means testing the final product more." — It emphasises prevention and monitoring at control points, reducing reliance on end-product testing [source: HACCP review].

Readings: [HACCP review], [HACCP barriers]

Lecture 9: Critical control points

Objective: Identify critical control points in a simple food process.

Hook (2 min): In making pasteurised milk, many steps matter, but one step — heating the milk to the right temperature for the right time — is the point where we can actually destroy harmful bacteria and prove it. Miss that step and nothing later fixes it. That special kind of step has a name: a critical control point. (Mark any specific temperature/time value with [source: ] rather than stating it from memory.)

Core content (~40 min):

A critical control point (CCP) is a step where control can be applied and is essential to prevent, eliminate, or reduce a hazard to an acceptable level. The key word is "essential": a control point is any useful step, but a CCP is one where, if control is lost, safety is genuinely at risk and no later step will catch it. Identifying CCPs is the second HACCP principle and depends entirely on the hazard analysis done first [source: HACCP review].

To find CCPs, teams commonly reason step by step through the process, often using a decision-tree style set of questions: Is there a hazard here? Is this step meant to control it? Will a later step remove it? This structured questioning keeps the number of CCPs sensible — not every step is critical, and calling everything a CCP makes the system unworkable [source: HACCP review]. In a simple process, CCPs often cluster around heat treatment, cooling, and steps where contamination cannot later be removed.

Once a CCP is set, it needs a critical limit (a measurable line, such as a minimum temperature and time), monitoring to check it in real time, and a corrective action if the limit is crossed. This is where the earlier barriers bite: monitoring and record-keeping demand trained people and discipline, which small units may lack [source: HACCP barriers]. So identifying a CCP is only useful if someone can actually measure it and act on it — the human system matters as much as the technical one [source: HACCP barriers].

In-class activity (10 min): Show a simple flow for one product (choose paneer, bottled buttermilk, or fried snacks). Groups mark each step as "control point" or "critical control point" and justify one CCP using the question: "Will a later step remove this hazard?" Each group defends one CCP to the class.

Self-check: 1. What makes a step a CCP rather than just a control point? — Control there is essential, and loss of control cannot be corrected by a later step. 2. Which HACCP principle covers CCP identification? — The second principle, after hazard analysis. 3. What three things does every CCP need? — A critical limit, monitoring, and a corrective action.

Common misconceptions: - "Every important step is a CCP." — Only steps essential to control a hazard, with no later step to fix it, are CCPs [source: HACCP review]. - "Once you name a CCP, the job is done." — It must be given a critical limit, monitored, and backed by corrective action and records [source: HACCP review].

Lecture 10: Codex and the global frame

Objective: Describe what Codex Alimentarius is and explain the roles of FAO and WHO in shaping food safety standards.

Hook (2 min): Think about the last packet of biscuits or bottle of oil you bought. Somewhere on it there was a claim about safety and quality. Now imagine that biscuit is exported from India to another country, or a mango from another country arrives in your local market. How do two countries agree on what "safe" means, when they have different kitchens, climates, and habits? That shared rulebook has a name, and today we meet it.

Core content (~40 min):

First beat — what Codex is. Codex Alimentarius is a collection of internationally agreed food standards, guidelines, and codes of practice. It is not a single law that punishes anyone; think of it as a common reference that countries can look to when they write their own rules. One of its most important pieces for us in this course is the General Principles of Food Hygiene, which lays out basic hygiene expectations from farm to plate [source: Codex hygiene]. The idea is simple: if everyone works from a shared foundation, food can move between countries with more trust and less confusion.

Second beat — who runs it and why two agencies. Codex is a joint effort supported by two United Nations bodies, the FAO (Food and Agriculture Organization) and the WHO (World Health Organization). Roughly speaking, FAO brings the agriculture and food-production side, while WHO brings the human-health and disease side. They provide the scientific advice and organisation behind the standards [source: Codex hygiene]. Bringing both together matters because a food safety decision is never only about the crop or the animal, and never only about the patient in a hospital — it is about both at once.

Third beat — why an Indian student should care. India both exports food (spices, rice, seafood, tea) and imports food, so Codex acts as a common language in trade. When our national rules line up with widely accepted principles, our products face fewer disputes abroad, and buyers here can trust what comes in. The Codex hygiene principles also feed into the everyday practices we will study — cleaning, temperature control, avoiding cross-contamination — which appear again in Indian training material [source: Codex hygiene]. So Codex is not a far-away document; its ideas quietly sit inside the checklists a dhaba or a dairy uses.

In-class activity (10 min): In pairs, pick one common Indian food that crosses borders (for example, basmati rice, frozen shrimp, or mango pulp). List two reasons a foreign buyer might worry about its safety, and next to each write which side of the problem is more "agriculture/FAO" and which is more "health/WHO". Share one pair's answer with the class.

Self-check: 1. Is Codex a law that fines companies? — No; it is a set of agreed standards and guidelines that countries use as a reference. 2. Name the two UN agencies behind Codex and one thing each brings. — FAO (agriculture/food-production side) and WHO (human-health side). 3. Why does Codex matter for Indian food exporters? — It provides a shared standard, so products face fewer disputes and more trust in trade.

Common misconceptions: - "Codex directly controls what shops in India can sell." — No; national authorities make binding rules, while Codex is a reference they may draw on. - "Codex is only about international trade, not hygiene basics." — It also sets out core hygiene principles used in everyday food handling [source: Codex hygiene].

Lecture 11: FSSAI and food law in India

Objective: Explain the role of FSSAI and describe its key requirements for food businesses.

Hook (2 min): Look closely at a packaged snack from a local shop and you will often spot a small logo with a licence number. That number is a promise that someone is accountable for what is inside. Who set up that system, and what does a food business actually have to do to earn that number? (If you wish to cite a real launch date or the exact logo history, verify it first — do not state a date from memory [source: FSSAI manual].)

Core content (~40 min):

First beat — what FSSAI is and what it does. FSSAI is India's national food safety authority. Its job is to set standards for food, to oversee that food businesses follow safety rules, and to build awareness and training so that safe practices actually happen on the ground [source: FSSAI manual]. It is helpful to see FSSAI as playing several roles at once: a standard-setter, a supervisor of the system, and an educator. The training material we read in this course exists precisely because rules on paper only work when handlers understand them [source: FSSAI guidance].

Second beat — licensing and registration. A central requirement is that food businesses must be registered or licensed to operate, depending on their size and nature. A small home-based seller and a large processing plant do not face identical paperwork, but everyone in the chain is expected to be on the record [source: FSSAI manual]. Along with this comes the expectation of basic hygiene and safety practices — clean premises, safe water, pest control, proper storage — which mirror the same hygiene ideas we met with Codex [source: FSSAI guidance]. If you want the exact categories of registration versus licence, check the manual rather than guessing [source: FSSAI manual].

Third beat — trained people and the food safety supervisor idea. Rules need trained humans behind them. Indian food safety training encourages that food handlers learn safe practices, and that businesses have a trained supervisor who can guide the team and keep standards up [source: FSSAI manual]. The training programme for food handlers is built around this idea of spreading practical, everyday knowledge — washing hands, separating raw and cooked food, controlling temperature — to the people who actually touch the food [source: FSSAI guidance]. This is where national law and the daily kitchen finally meet.

In-class activity (10 min): Imagine you are opening a small tea-and-snacks stall near campus. On a worksheet, list five things FSSAI would likely expect from you before and during operation (for example, being registered, keeping the area clean, using safe water). Mark any item you are unsure about with a "?" so we can check it against the reading together [source: FSSAI manual].

Self-check: 1. Name two roles FSSAI plays in the food system. — Setting standards and supervising/educating food businesses (any two of: standard-setter, supervisor, educator). 2. Do all food businesses face the same paperwork? — No — requirements differ by size and nature (registration versus licence) [source: FSSAI manual]. 3. Why is training food handlers a legal and practical priority? — Because written rules only protect people when handlers understand and apply them [source: FSSAI guidance].

Common misconceptions: - "Only big factories need FSSAI approval." — Even small sellers are expected to be registered or licensed; the level differs by size [source: FSSAI manual]. - "Once you have a licence, hygiene is optional." — The licence assumes ongoing hygiene and safe practices, not a one-time formality [source: FSSAI guidance].

Lecture 12: Putting it together (capstone)

Objective: Evaluate a real food operation and propose control measures, drawing on all four weeks of the course.

Hook (2 min): Picture a busy sweet shop during a festival like Diwali — trays of milk-based sweets on the counter, a crowd, one tap at the back, and the afternoon heat. It looks festive and normal. But as trained eyes, we can now walk through that same shop and spot where safety could quietly break down. Today you become the inspector. (If you reference a real festival-season food incident, verify the details first — do not state a specific outbreak or number from memory [source: FSSAI manual].)

Core content (~40 min):

First beat — the evaluation mindset. Evaluating a food operation is not about finding someone to blame; it is about spotting where a hazard could enter and asking whether there is a control in place. Across this course we have built a toolkit: hazards can be biological, chemical, or physical; hygiene principles guide handling from source to plate [source: Codex hygiene]; and India's system expects registration, clean premises, and trained people [source: FSSAI manual]. A good evaluation walks the food's journey — receiving, storage, preparation, holding, serving — and checks each step.

Second beat — from problem to control. Once you spot a weak point, the next move is proposing a control measure that is realistic for that business. For our sweet shop, a milk-based sweet left in the heat is a temperature-control problem; a control might be keeping it cold or limiting how long it sits out [source: Codex hygiene]. One tap shared for cleaning and washing hands is a cross-contamination and hygiene problem; a control might be separate handwashing and clear cleaning routines [source: FSSAI guidance]. The skill is matching each hazard to a control that a small Indian business can actually follow.

Third beat — connecting the four weeks. This capstone is where earlier ideas lock together. The concept of hazards and how contamination spreads (early weeks) tells you what to look for; the hygiene principles (Codex) tell you the standard; and the Indian legal frame (FSSAI) tells you who is accountable and what training should exist [source: Codex hygiene] [source: FSSAI manual]. When you can look at any real operation — a dairy, a canteen, a street cart — and move smoothly from "what could go wrong" to "here is a practical control", you have met the goal of this course.

In-class activity (10 min): Structured evaluation exercise. Working in small groups, take one operation (assigned: a milk vendor, a college canteen, a wedding caterer, or a fruit-juice cart). Fill a four-column table: (1) the step in the food's journey, (2) the possible hazard, (3) whether a control seems present, (4) your proposed control measure. Each group must draw on at least one idea from each earlier week and cite the relevant reading beside at least two rows [source: FSSAI guidance] [source: Codex hygiene]. Groups present their single most important finding.

Self-check: 1. What are the three broad types of hazard you scan for? — Biological, chemical, and physical. 2. What two things should a good control measure balance? — Actually reducing the hazard, and being realistic for that specific business to follow. 3. In an evaluation, why walk the food's whole journey instead of checking one step? — Because hazards can enter at any step — receiving, storage, preparation, holding, or serving.

Common misconceptions: - "Evaluation means catching and punishing the owner." — It is about finding weak points and fixing them with practical controls, not blame. - "A control measure is only valid if it is expensive or high-tech." — Simple, low-cost steps like temperature control and separate handwashing are often the most effective [source: Codex hygiene].
