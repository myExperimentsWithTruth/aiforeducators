AI for Educators · Session 3 · The NotebookLM ecosystem

Explore the course you just built — grounded, and cited

Yesterday's run-sheet prompts produced the Introduction to Food Safety course pack. Today we load it into NotebookLM and explore it — and every answer, quiz and summary points back to a source. This is the scenario to run live on the shared screen.
The one idea. A plain chatbot answers from everything it ever read and can invent a fact or a citation. NotebookLM answers only from the sources you give it, and cites every sentence. Same question, but now the answer is anchored to your material — that is grounding, and it is why hallucination drops to near zero.
Part AThe ecosystem — three panels

NotebookLM is one screen with three parts. Everything flows left to right: your sources feed the chat and the studio, and nothing is generated that is not drawn from those sources.

Sources

Left — what it knows

The material you upload. The model is walled inside this set — it will not reach outside it.

  • PDFs, Google Docs & Slides
  • Copied text / Markdown
  • Web pages & YouTube links
  • Audio files
  • Free tier: up to ~50 sources per notebook

Chat

Centre — ask & verify

Ask questions in plain language. Answers come back with numbered citations you click to jump to the exact passage in a source.

  • Grounded Q&A
  • Inline, clickable citations
  • Says so when the answer is not in the sources
  • Save any answer as a note

Studio

Right — generate artifacts

One click turns the sources into teaching material — all cited. This is where study guides, quizzes and audio come from.

  • Reports, Quiz, Flashcards
  • Mind Map
  • Audio & Video Overview
  • Data Table (Pro)
Part BWhat the Studio can make
ReportsStudy guides, briefing docs, FAQs, timelines — or a custom format you describe.
QuizMultiple-choice and short-answer questions with cited answers; you can ask for a difficulty level.
FlashcardsQuestion-and-answer cards on the key terms, each tracing to a passage.
Mind MapA visual tree of how the ideas connect — good for showing course structure.
Audio OverviewA spoken, podcast-style summary of the sources — the crowd-pleaser.
Video OverviewA narrated visual walkthrough of the material.
Data Table ProExtracts structured data into a table you can export to Sheets.
NotesSave answers and outputs; pin them as new sources to build on.
Part CThe live scenario — explore the Food Safety pack

Run these steps on the shared screen; participants repeat each on their own notebook. The whole point: watch the answers stay inside the sources, and watch the [source: …] gaps in the draft get filled with real, cited facts.

1

Create the notebook and add the sources

Sources

New notebook, then name it Introduction to Food Safety. Upload the course pack (the 12-lecture document) and the readings from the pack — WHO, Codex, the MDPI reviews, the FSSAI manuals. Point out the source count on the left: this, and only this, is what it now knows.

2

Ask a grounded question — and click the citation

Chat
Type into chatWhat are the three families of food hazards, and give one example of each from the course?
Show: the answer comes back with little numbered citations. Click one — it jumps to the exact line in Lecture 3 or 6. That link is the difference between trust and guesswork.
3

Fill a [source: …] gap with a real, cited fact

Chat

The draft Lecture 1 says "hundreds of millions fall ill each year [source: WHO fact sheet]" — a placeholder we would not teach unverified. Now close it:

Type into chatAccording to the WHO fact sheet, roughly how many people fall ill from unsafe food each year, and how many die? Quote the figure and cite it.
Do: read back the cited number, click through to the WHO source to confirm, and replace the [source: …] marker in the lecture with the verified figure. This is the habit — the gap becomes a grounded fact.
4

Run the trap question

Chat
Type into chatWhat percentage of foodborne deaths in India are caused specifically by street food?
Watch: that figure is not in the sources, so it declines or says it cannot find it — instead of inventing a number. That refusal is the whole value of grounding. Contrast it with what a plain chatbot would confidently make up.
5

Generate a Study Guide and a Briefing doc

Studio, then Reports

Studio, then Reports, then Study Guide for the whole pack — it returns key terms, review questions and short answers, each cited. Then generate a Briefing doc you could hand a co-teacher. Point out: every claim carries a citation, so a student can always check it.

6

Generate a Quiz and Flashcards

Studio, then Quiz / Flashcards

Studio, then Quiz — ask for ten questions at an introductory level; every answer traces to a source passage. Then Flashcards on the key terms (hazard, risk, cross-contamination, adulterant, CCP, HACCP). Open one quiz answer's citation to prove it is grounded, not guessed.

7

Draw the Mind Map

Studio, then Mind Map

Studio, then Mind Map — it lays out the course as a tree: the scale of the problem, the three hazard types, hygiene and HACCP, and Indian regulation. A one-glance picture of how the twelve lectures hang together, built from your own pack.

8

Play the Audio Overview — the wow

Studio, then Audio Overview

Studio, then Audio Overview — generate a short spoken summary of the course and play thirty seconds live. Make the point: this is your material, spoken back, grounded — a revision aid a student can listen to on the bus.

9

Close the loop

Habit
Leave the room with this: Gemini created the draft fast, but with [source: …] gaps; NotebookLM grounded it and gave you cited study guides, quizzes and audio you can hand a student. Verify one citation before you trust any output — and only ever load sources you are allowed to use.
